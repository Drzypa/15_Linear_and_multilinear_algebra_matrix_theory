proof of determinant of the Vandermonde matrix


To begin, note that the determinant of the n×n Vandermonde matrix (which we shall denote as ‘Δ’) is a homogeneous polynomial of order n⁢(n-1)/2 because every term in the determinant is, up to sign, the product of a zeroth power of some variable times the first power of some other variable , …, the n-1-st power of some variable and 0+1+⋯+(n-1)=n⁢(n-1)/2.

Next, note that if ai=aj with i≠j, then Δ=0 because two columns of the matrix would be equal. Since Δ is a polynomial, this implies that ai-aj is a factor of Δ. Hence,

Δ=C⁢∏1≤i<j≤n(aj-ai)

where C is some polynomial. However, since both Δ and the product on the right hand side have the same degree, C must have degree zero, i.e. C must be a constant. So all that remains is the determine the value of this constant.

One way to determine this constant is to look at the coefficient of the leading diagonal, ∏n(an)n-1. Since it equals 1 in both the determinant and the product, we conclude that C=1, hence

Δ=∏1≤i<j≤n(aj-ai).
