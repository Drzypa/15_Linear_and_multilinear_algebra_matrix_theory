strain transformation


Let E be a Euclidean plane. Fix a line ℓ in E and a real number r≠0. Take any point p∈E. Drop a line mp from p perpendicular to ℓ. Denote d⁢(p,ℓ) the distance from p to ℓ. Then there is a unique point p′ on mp such that

d⁢(p′,ℓ)=r⋅d⁢(p,ℓ).

The function sr:E→E such that sr⁢(p)=p′ is called a strain transformation, or simply a strain.

One can visualize a strain stretches a geometric figure if |r|>1 and compresses it if |r|<1. If r=1, then sr is the identity function, the only time when a strain is a rigid motion. Again, if ℓ is the x-axis, then sr is the function that sends (x,y) to (x,r⁢y). Representing the ordered pairs as column vectors and sr as a matrix , we have

sr⁢(xy)=(100r)⁢(xy)=(xr⁢y).

Nevertheless, a strain, as a (non-singular) linear transformation, takes lines to lines, and parallel lines to parallel lines.

In general, given any finite dimensional vector space V over a field k, a strain sr is a non-singular diagonalizable linear transformation on V such that sr leaves a subspace W of codimension 1 fixed. 0≠r∈k is called the strain coefficient.

Remark. By choosing an appropriate base for V of dimension n, sr can be represented as a diagonal matrix whose diagonals are 1 in at least n-1 cells and r in at most one cell.

It is easy to see that every non-singular diagonalizable linear transformation on V can be written as a product of n strains, where n=dim⁡(V).
